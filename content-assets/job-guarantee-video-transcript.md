## Segment 2: The Unemployment/Inflation Trade-Off (0:30–1:50) — Patricia Pino

> **SHORTS CANDIDATE #1: "The Dirty Secret of Inflation Control"**
> Timestamps: 0:30–1:50 (80s, trim to 60s)
> Hook: Provocative framing most people don't know. High share potential.
> Key quote: *"Deliberately allowing unemployment to rise is central to how inflation is controlled in a neoliberal economy."*

Today, the main way governments manage inflation is by raising and lowering interest rates. What most people don't realize is that this system involves our policy makers effectively building in and maintaining a certain level of unemployment in the economy.

Policy makers understand it like this: when unemployment goes up, workers have less bargaining power and wages grow more slowly, thereby reducing the risk of inflation. When unemployment goes down, workers have more bargaining power and wages tend to rise faster, which increases the risk of inflation.

This is rarely discussed in public discourse, but deliberately allowing unemployment to rise is central to how inflation is controlled in a neoliberal economy.

## Segment 3: The Human Cost (1:51–2:09) — Patricia Pino

The problem with this paradigm is that it takes for granted that some people must be permanently excluded from economic life. But unemployment is not just a statistic. It imposes a real and lasting human and societal costs.

Without access to jobs, people face social exclusion and risk long-term harm to their own wellbeing, and that of their communities. Using unemployment to manage prices is not only socially harmful, it is also a very inefficient way to stabilize the economy.

## Segment 4: Efficiency & Social Costs (2:09–4:46) — Pavlina Tcherneva

> **SHORTS CANDIDATE #5: "Prevention, Not Cure"**
> Timestamps: 3:36–4:46 (70s)
> Hook: Mental health + crime framing. Emotionally resonant.
> Key quote: *"We don't have preventative solutions. We always allow unemployment to develop, and then we scratch our heads."*

My name is Pavlina Tcherneva. I'm a professor of economics at Bard College, the president of the Levy Economics Institute, and I've authored in 2020 *The Case for a Job Guarantee*, which outlines the economic case and the various benefits for the program. But I've worked also at the policy level studying real life employment programs and working with governments on thinking about what kind of design features programs as this might have.

On the efficiency question — you know, we have so narrowly defined a society's efficiency as something that falls in this worldview that as long as there's some kind of return and usually a financial return to that activity, then that's what makes it useful. But in fact, if you really look at what makes a good society, we would recognize that there's so many employment opportunities, so many jobs on which we rely that are not there for profit, that are absolutely fundamental to the smooth functioning of a society.

So we can think about employment in healthcare. We can think about employment in climate work, in various in education, where the returns might not be visible. They may not be immediately financial — they're returns to the greater wellbeing of the society. And so we really need to be thinking in broader terms about what makes the good life and what kind of employment is required to support the well functioning of an economy.

And I think the final one that's really worth mentioning is that a lot of social problems are related to the absence of decent work. We know very well from research that mental and physical health deteriorates quite rapidly for those who don't have stable or any employment. We know that there's such a thing as economic crime — petty theft and other problems. So not only are people sicker and weaker, but that creates the very conditions that might prevent them from then taking employment subsequently.

So the job guarantee is really effective at addressing those social costs, but not only eliminating them, but also preventing them. And I think this is one thing that's missing in our overall approach to economic problems. We don't have preventative solutions. We always allow unemployment to develop, and then we scratch our heads, and then we think, what can we do to bring those people back into the labour market — as opposed to simply have an open-ended employment opportunity and then prevent the problems associated, especially with long-term unemployment.

## Segment 5: What a Job Guarantee Does (4:47–5:14) — Patricia Pino

A job guarantee offers a much more humane and effective way to stabilize the economy. Instead of controlling inflation through unemployment, the government links spending directly to productive work by offering a job to anyone who wants one.

With a job guarantee, involuntary unemployment is effectively eliminated. Anyone willing and able to work is able to access a job providing a minimum level of income and security.

## Segment 6: Wage Floor & Conditions (5:14–6:01) — Patricia Pino

> **SHORTS CANDIDATE #6: "Not Your Average Jobs Scheme"** (combines with Segment 8)
> Timestamps for Segment 8: 9:12–10:01 (49s)
> Hook: UK-specific. Distinguishes JG from Labour's criticised schemes.

The wage for this job should be set at a level that supports a minimum decent standard of living in our society. And for this purpose, we can use respected benchmarks such as the Joseph Rowntree Foundation's minimum income standard.

The program establishes a wage floor across the whole economy, so workers are no longer forced to accept low pay, unsafe or precarious working conditions just to get by. This is especially important for people in the gig economy who are still in zero hours contracts or in other insecure and poorly protected forms of work.

To retain workers, employers must offer pay and conditions that are at least as good as the job guarantee alternative, while those who prefer to stay in these kinds of jobs for flexibility purposes remain free to do so.

## Segment 7: Argentina's Plan Jefe (6:02–9:12) — Daniel Kostzer

> **SHORTS CANDIDATE #2: "Argentina Tried It"**
> Timestamps: 6:02–8:30 (148s, trim to 90s or split into 2 parts)
> Hook: Real-world case study. 25% unemployment, 55% poverty. First-person authority.

My name is Daniel Kostzer. I am an economist. I am actually the chief economist of the International Trade Union Confederation, and I have a long experience in labor market issues.

In relation to more precisely to job guarantee, I was in the team that drafted the first employment guarantee program in Argentina that was called the Plan Jefe y Jefa. That was the plan implemented after the crisis in 2002, 2003 in Argentina.

Argentina underwent in the nineties a very typical neoliberal Washington consensus type of policies that eroded the productive capacity of the country, that reduced the formal employment, increased unemployment significantly. At the same time, that also increased poverty.

By the end of 2001, the level of unemployment was almost 25%. Poverty was above 55%, and the unrest was so massive that induced social protests that ended up with the changes in governments and also the need of a major public intervention in order to reduce the crisis.

**Q: So what specifically are you trying to achieve with the program?**

With the program that was, as I mentioned, what we were trying was to cover a large and increasing unemployment in the country where we saw that month after month, the number of people looking for a job and without having a job was increasing. But also we saw that many people were what we may call discouraged workers, that they didn't even go to look for a job because they found no opportunities.

So the objective was to provide the job in order to transfer an income to these households, to these people, and reduce poverty and unemployment rate, which are very sensitive indicators of the political distress of the population.

**Q: And what were the biggest challenges you found when introducing the program?**

Well, the challenges are at different levels. The first challenge is to convince politicians that you are giving something that's more called like an entitlement rather than a traditional social policy of targeting and allocating to different groups. So that was the first thing.

The second challenge was to try to assess how many people were going to go into the program. Another challenge was how to organize the people that would go into the program to avoid the political patronizing of allocating to this one yes, and to this other no — just try to make it universal.

And then the other challenge was to find activities that would be useful for the community.

## Segment 8: UK Programs Compared (9:12–10:01) — Patricia Pino

> **SHORTS CANDIDATE #6: "Not Your Average Jobs Scheme"**
> Timestamps: 9:12–10:01 (49s)
> Hook: UK-specific. Distinguishes JG from Labour's criticised schemes.

In the UK, some programs have been described as job guarantees in the past, such as Labour's youth guarantee, for example. But this differs significantly from a universal public job guarantee.

To begin with, people are often forced to take part, risking the loss of benefits if they refuse jobs that they did not choose, and are given little chance to gain useful experience or improve their long-term prospects. They're also not universally available, usually restricted by quotas, and often paying only the legal minimum wage, which many agree is not enough to meet basic living standards.

Worse still, many of these schemes allow for-profit companies to access subsidized labor. This creates a risk of displacing existing non-subsidized workers rather than creating new socially useful work.

> **SHORTS CANDIDATE #3: "Every Country Can Afford This"**

> Key quote: *"The best social policy at a very reasonable cost in terms of fiscal space needed."*

I would say every single country could afford this type of program. Number one, because they're counter cyclical. That's the best idea of this type of program — to have it always implemented. So you have like a buffer stock of jobs that are available when the economy goes down, people move from the private sector to this type of program, and then when the economy goes up, they move to the other side of the market.

So if they work as a buffer stock, you will never need huge amounts of money because it will be precisely to reduce the cycle — like many economies point out as one of the objectives of this type of program.

So I don't think that the cost of a program is the main topic. They are, I would say, the best social policy at a very reasonable cost in terms of the fiscal space needed.

## Segment 10: Green Jobs & Climate (11:45–13:05) — Pavlina Tcherneva

> **SHORTS CANDIDATE #4: "Jobs That Actually Matter"**
> Timestamps: 11:45–13:05 (80s)
> Hook: Climate angle gives broader appeal. Tangible examples.

The job guarantee is simply put a direct employment approach. It is an approach that employs those who are looking for good jobs in positions that do public service or address challenges such as the climate challenges.

The job guarantee is conceived as a broader program for employment, one that will be redirected to various social needs. They could be care needs, they could be environmental needs, they could be community needs. But I think that probably the most urgent threat we all have to address as society is the climate threat. And that ripples through our communities, that affects the way we care for each other. That affects the way our towns and cities can be livable communities.

And so the job guarantee essentially thinks through the kind of investments that are necessary for climate work, and then creates those opportunities. We can think of small projects like greening our cities. You can think of trees as the lungs of our towns and our communities. We can think about community gardens that provide locally sourced food that is not dependent on supply chains across the globe and produce sustainable quality food for everyone.

We can think of large scale investments — installation of solar panels, wind turbines, various other solutions to energy production. So we can think very broadly of what is required. And the job guarantee doesn't rely on incentives waiting around for the private sector to figure out the precise return in order to direct its activities in that area. It is a very direct and straightforward approach to the problem. In fact, two problems — the problem of unemployment and the problem of the green transition.

## Segment 11: Local Delivery & Community-Driven Work (13:05–14:46) — Patricia Pino

A universal public job guarantee is fundamentally different. It's nationally funded and delivered locally. So communities decide what work is needed and valuable in their areas.

Funding expands where more people need jobs and contracts as the economy recovers, directing support exactly where and when it is required. Instead of paying the high social costs of unemployment, we invest in useful work, making better use of public resources while strengthening local economies.

Local priorities shape job creation, making the program more responsive and democratic.

The program is open to nonprofits, cooperatives, and social enterprises that meet clear standards. All jobs follow national and local guidelines to ensure that they provide real community benefit, build skills, and do not replace existing public sector roles or unfairly compete with well-provisioned commercial activity.

Most importantly, people themselves can take the lead. Individuals or even small teams of up to five people can propose and deliver projects that address local needs with support from their local authorities.

The result is practical, community-driven work that unlocks local talent, strengthens neighborhoods, and ensures that everyone can access decent, meaningful employment.

In the age of AI, that has become more important than ever.
